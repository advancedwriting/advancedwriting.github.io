JMLR: Getting Published in the Premier Machine Learning Journal

June 13, 2018

About me

Eric Liu is a third year PhD student from Avon, CT in the Electrical and Computer Engineering Department. At Northeastern, he is a member of the SMILE (Synergetic Media Learning) Lab. His research is focused on transfer learning of language understanding using semi/unsupervised learning recurrent neural networks.

JMLR: Getting Published in the Premier Machine Learning Journal 

Starting out in 2000, JMLR or the Journal of Machine Learning Research, is one of the top machine learning journals in the world. Spun off from the journal Machine Learning, JMLR aims to be open, with every publication available for free online (print versions can also be purchased). Aside from their yearly publications, JMLR also publishes collections of special topics. I was very fortunate to have been published in the most recent edition for my work in improving state of the art performance in natural language understanding using unsupervised transfer learning. In this blog, I hope to share some tips and hopefully spread interest in the field of machine learning!

TThe process of getting a paper accepted is quite long and arduous indeed. After initial submission, a paper can take up to six months or more before hearing back. Longer waiting times could often time be a good thing though. New papers are first sent to the editor-in-chief who gives it a quick scan and decides if it should continue to an action editor (expert in the area of your paper). The action editor then decides if your paper should continue on to three more reviewers each of whom are chosen by the action editor. The action editor then waits to collect the reviewers’ feedback and at anytime can choose to accept or reject your paper. All editors can be found here (http://www.jmlr.org/editorial-board.html) and are all expert researchers from universities around the world. As a forewarning, you should expect extra delays in your review if your paper is extremely complex or if it is during the time of review for other large conferences. Don’t fret too much if your paper is rejected! Often times if your paper is almost there but not quite, the action editor will suggest you to resubmit your paper with changes. You should definitely clear up any confusion with your reviewers.

JMLR has extremely high technical standards. Papers are often 40 pages long, filled with highly complicated formulas and proofs. The journal’s standards have definitely increased dramatically since it began. This can be definitely attributed to the explosion of interest in machine learning especially in the most recent years. The number of papers included in the yearly publication has also grown. With just 62 and 61 publications in 2003 and 2002 respectively, the journal more now more than has seen 2-4x more papers per year with 118 and 236 papers in 2017 and 2016 respectively. Published authors include those from highly regarded institutions as well as industry leaders (Google, Facebook, etc). The details of how to format and prepare your paper are too in depth to be described in this post, instead they can be found https://www.jmlr.org/format/format.html and https://www.jmlr.org/format/authors-guide.html.

The scope of JMLR is quite broad, ranging from new algorithms to well-written surveys of existing work. For those of you who are familiar with the leading conferences in the field of machine learning, the topics covered in JMLR are broader than that of CVPR (Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition) and NIPS (Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems) which focus on computer vision and neural networks respectively. It is more so like ICML (International Conference of Machine Learning) which cover not just deep learning but a very broad overview of any interesting research in the field of machine learning. Reading through the proceedings it may seem like there is a bias towards deep learning papers though this is primarily due to the interest in deep learning currently. I bring up these prestigious conferences as a strong tip of mine is to use your strong papers to apply for both JMLR and these conferences at the same time! JMLR, however, does expect you to go much more in depth into your research which is not the case for the conference papers. This could include adding proofs, extensions of experiments, or extra background and references. Being accepted into a prestigious conference and JMLR with the same research will definitely boost your career in academia.

If you are curious about my paper, is it called “Distributed Sequence Memory of Multidimensional Inputs in Recurrent Networks” and can be found here http://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume18/16-270/16-270.pdf. Without going into too much detail, it covers an analysis on a structural variation of the traditional recurrent neural network structure called an echo state network. It definitely fits in with the trend of deep learning research these past few years. For me, the paper took about five months to get through.

The above advice applies to current graduate students as well as driven undergraduates! If you are looking to get involved in research, undergrad is an amazing time to start. SMILE is always looking for help and you can contact me directly at liu.che@husky.neu.edu or our PI at yunfu@ece.neu.edu. Other groups on campus are also doing amazing work including Jennifer Dy’s group (http://www.ece.neu.edu/fac-ece/jdy/), the applied machine learning group (https://www.ccis.northeastern.edu/group/applied-machine-learning-group/), and many more. I encourage any of you interested in the field of machine learning to join groups at Northeastern and learn about your passions.
